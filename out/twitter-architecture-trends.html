About Twitter

Twitter is an online service using 280-characters-long messages (named tweets) for two primary roles:
  • Social media: Users can share their own content and consume the one created by others.
  • Social networking: Users can build their network by connecting with other users sharing the same interests.
Twitter was created by Jack Dorsey, Noah Glass, Biz Stone, and Evan Williams in March 2006 and launched in the next year. It grew from 50 millions users in 2010 to 300 millions in 2015. In the following years, its usage slowly increased to 400 millions in 2022. In the same year, Elon Musk bought all shares (TWTR, listed at New York Stock Exchange) in the company, that became in this way a privately-held one.
 
 

Modus Operandi

Twitter can be considered a consumption platform. In 2013, Raffi Krikorian, the VP of Engineering at the moment, stated that there were 300000 (read) queries and only 6000 writes, all in one second. This characteristic of Twitter is further strengthened by some statistics from 2022: 400 millions monthly active users, with 60 billions tweets in a month. This means that there are (in average) 15 tweets per month. The rest of the 3 hours (6 minutes daily) that are not spent by writing (280-characters-long) tweets is spent reading other tweets.
This characteristic is relevant for architectural reasons: the read path of the service-oriented architecture needs to be optimized, with a possible performance compromise on the writing path.

Architectural Evolution

Analyzing multiple resources from the Internet (linked in the last section), the following list of architectural observations can be built. Their purpose is not to give a theoretical model of why they made those decisions, but to deduct the first principles and support them with concrete examples.

Making Product Features More Important Than Architectural Perfection

Multiple engineers, including the ones that were fired after the recent layoff, stated that the source code of Twitter is in a completely unstable, but functional state. It ensures the functioning of the application, but too much technical debt was accumulated.
It’s not sure if this practice continued after 2013, but Raffi Krikorian affirmed in its presentation at InfoQ that there was a dedicated team for designing a holistic architecture. Another purpose of the team was to maintain a list of technical debt, which they wanted to resolve gradually.
This depicts a common theme in the companies with explosive growth, namely prioritizing product features over architectural perfection. The decision may lead to scars, in the form of debt, but the company’s purpose (to keep the users involved and happy with its services) is still accomplished.

Migrating from Monoliths to SOA

In the same presentation from 2013, Raffi presented a diagram with an architecture in which a monolithic service, named Monorail, cover up three layers: routing, presentation, and logic. This is due to the old web stack that they used for their launch, but he stated that a goal of its team is to split up the service into smaller, isolated services. This change was supposed to ensure better speed, reliability and development speed.
Evidence of the continuous architectural progress was found in the 2013 presentation from a tech lead at Tweet Service team, Jeremy Cloud.
In 19 November 2022, Elon Musk, the new CEO, tweeted a picture containing two architectures: the current and the desired one. There can be observed that the Monorail service is gone, being replaced by smaller, more niched services.

Choosing the Best Technology for Their Needs

Besides its size, the Ruby on Rails monolith suffered from another disadvantage: the programming language is not optimized for concurrency, that is mandatory for scaling the service in accordance with the growth in usage.
In 2013, Jeremy Cloud stated that some JVM services running Scala are present on the presentation and logic layers. This denotes that the engineers doesn’t fear to relinquish a technology in favor of another one, only to accomplish better scalability.
This aspect is further strengthened by another observed change. Before 2010, Twitter used only MySQL for storing relevant data, such as users information and timelines. In 2010, they leveraged an in-house framework named Gizzard, that allowed them to replicate MySQL databases to multiple servers. But after that year, the standard structured databases did not comply anymore with their requirements, so they implied advanced storage technologies such as caching (with Redis), searching (with Lucine), data clustering (with Hadoop), and binary databases (with Blobstore, an in-house software for storing binary data such as users’ images).

Obtaining Physical Layer Independence

Twitter was operated before 2010 on a rented infrastructure of a third-party hosting provider. They made the decision to become more independent on the physical layer, so the same year give birth to the first design of the on-premise network. Fast-forward, 2015 found Twitter with points of presence on five continents and data centers with hundreds of thousands of servers.
In 2018, it was announced that a collaboration with Google Cloud will create Partly Cloud: a hybrid infrastructure where parts of the data platform (which, between others, ensured analytical capabilities) and some Hadoop clusters are migrated to Google Cloud Platform.

Making Changes Gradually

Developers adapted an Agile approach by deploying often and incrementally, where deploys consisted in making small changes, verifying them in production (with small batches of users), making adjustments in case of malfunctioning and repeating. Another resilience technique was detailed by Jeremy Cloud and consisted of implementing a dark mode for each feature, namely the ability to disable it in critical conditions (for example, service fail).

Relying on Third-Parties for Specialized Software

Twitter builds a lot of in-house software, some of them being publically hosted on their GitHub organization. But there are cases in which the engineers don’t need to invest time into a specific problem: not because they can’t build the required software, but that the effort is wasted for a problem not aligned with companies revenue streams.
An example is again the migration of the data platform to Google Cloud. The latter already offers, in its software-as-a-service suite, mature tools for data analysis and machine learning: BigQuery, an enterprise data warehouse with a SQL engine, and Looker Studio (known before December 2022 as Data Studio), a big data visualization tool.
Another migration to third-party software is announced in 2021. Before then, Twitter used an in-house developed solution, named Logles. The main issue was performance-related: only 10% of the submitted logs ended up to be stored on disk, the rest being dropped due to rate limits.
They successfully migrated to Splunk Enterprise, which offered them a 400% increase in the log processing power, better configurability, a robust API and new advanced features (for example, scheduled searches). The logging architecture consisted in forwarders, installed on each server, and special ones (based on rsyslog), that receives logging messages from network equipments.

Future Changes

It’s unclear what will happen with Twitter’s architecture in the following years. The only thing that we, as outsiders, can do is to watch the last months' evolution and to (try to) predict the future.
After Elon’s arrival, multiple features were added, the most notables being Twitter Blue, long-form tweets, and an edit feature for tweets. Besides this, some events contributed to altering the reputation of the platform: outage produced by changes in the backend architecture and information leaks, where millions of users emails were stolen by black-hat hackers.
But to orient the discussion to the future, an opinion to consider is Dave Troy’s one. He states that Elon’s plan is to slow down the development of the current technology and to free the bird from the closed-source cage.
In this way, Twitter will presumably use Bluesky’s The AT Protocol, an open and decentralized standard for social media. Bluesky was announced in 2019 by Jack Dorsey and is independent by Twitter, despite the 13 million dollar investment to continue its research.

Conclusions

To deduct details about the architecture of a private company can be compared with open-source intelligence (OSINT) work. You can’t know precisily if the facts are in black or white, but you can find evidence of things being in a specific way.
Regarding Twitter’s one, it’s still unclear how it look like, but we can now make a supposition having the presented information in mind. In any case, it’s impressive to see the scale it reached and to deduct architectural principles that were being used during the time.
